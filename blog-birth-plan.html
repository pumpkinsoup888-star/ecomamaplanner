How to Write a Natural
Birth Plan

A step-by-step guide to writing a clear, flexible birth plan that helps you communicate your wishes to your care team.

📅 March 2026 ⏱ 5 min read 🌿 EcoMamaPlanner

A birth plan — or birth preferences document — is one of the most empowering things you can prepare before your labour. It's a simple, clear document that communicates your wishes to your midwives, doctors, and support people, so that in the intensity of labour you don't have to find words for things you've already thought through. It's not a contract; it's a conversation starter.

"A birth plan isn't about controlling the uncontrollable. It's about knowing your options, articulating your values, and feeling prepared."

Before you start writing

The best birth plans come from genuine research and reflection, not from copying a template online. Before you write anything, consider attending a prenatal education class, reading about the stages of labour, and having an honest conversation with your midwife or obstetrician about what's realistically possible in your birth setting. A birth plan that works within your care environment is far more useful than one that conflicts with standard protocols.

What to include

Your support people

Who do you want present during labour and birth? Your partner, a doula, your mother? Be specific. Also note if there are people you do not want present during certain stages.

Pain management preferences

This is the most important section of most birth plans. Note your preferences in order: for example, "I would like to try a water birth, TENS machine, and gas first before considering an epidural." Being clear that you're open to changing your mind removes pressure and keeps communication open.

Mobility and positions

Note that you'd like to be free to move, walk, use a birth pool, or adopt positions that feel right. Research shows that upright, mobile positions in labour are associated with shorter labours and lower rates of intervention.

Interventions

Note your preferences around monitoring (intermittent vs. continuous), induction, episiotomy, and assisted delivery (forceps or vacuum). You might write: "Please discuss all interventions with me before proceeding unless there is an emergency."

Immediately after birth

This section matters enormously. Note your wishes for delayed cord clamping (waiting until the cord stops pulsing before cutting — associated with significant benefits for your baby), skin-to-skin contact immediately after birth, and who cuts the cord.

Feeding preferences

Note your intention to breastfeed and request support from a lactation consultant in the early hours. If breastfeeding doesn't work out as planned, note how you'd like to handle supplementation.

Keeping it simple and flexible

The most effective birth plans are one page, maximum. Use bullet points rather than paragraphs. Use language that's collaborative rather than demanding — "I would prefer" and "please discuss with me" rather than "I refuse." Include a note that you understand birth is unpredictable and that you trust your care team to keep you and your baby safe.

Share your birth plan with your midwife at around 34–36 weeks, and keep a few printed copies in your hospital bag. Then let it go — you've done the thinking, and now you can focus on trusting your body and your team.
