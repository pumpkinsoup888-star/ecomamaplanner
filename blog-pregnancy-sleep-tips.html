Pregnancy Sleep Tips —
How to Actually Rest

Evidence-backed strategies to help you sleep better in every trimester — plus the products that genuinely make a difference.

Sleep during pregnancy sounds simple — you're exhausted, you lie down, you sleep. If only it were that easy. The reality for most pregnant women is that sleep becomes progressively more difficult as pregnancy advances, with the third trimester often feeling like a cruel joke. You're more tired than you've ever been in your life, and yet deep, restorative sleep feels completely out of reach.

The good news is that there are genuinely effective strategies — both practical and product-based — that can transform your sleep during pregnancy. This guide covers everything you need to know, trimester by trimester.

"Sleep is not a luxury during pregnancy — it is medicine. Protecting your rest is one of the most important things you can do for yourself and your baby."

Why is sleep so difficult during pregnancy?

Poor sleep during pregnancy is not just about physical discomfort — though that certainly plays a role. Multiple physiological and hormonal changes contribute to disrupted sleep throughout all three trimesters:

  • First trimester: Surging progesterone causes extreme fatigue and drowsiness during the day but can paradoxically disrupt night sleep. Frequent urination, nausea, breast tenderness, and anxiety about the new pregnancy all compete for your attention at 2am.
  • Second trimester: Sleep often improves somewhat in the middle trimester — the nausea has typically eased, the bump isn't yet too large, and energy levels improve. However, vivid and disturbing pregnancy dreams become common, and back pain begins to emerge.
  • Third trimester: This is where sleep truly suffers. The growing bump makes comfortable positioning nearly impossible, frequent urination returns with a vengeance, heartburn is often at its worst, restless legs syndrome affects many pregnant women, and anxiety about the approaching birth can trigger insomnia.

The most important thing: sleep position

Before we get into tips and products, let's address the most common sleep question in pregnancy — which position is safe?

From around 20 weeks, healthcare providers generally recommend sleeping on your left side rather than your back or right side. Here's why: sleeping on your back from mid-pregnancy can compress the inferior vena cava — the large vein that returns blood from your lower body to your heart — reducing blood flow to both you and your baby. The left side is preferred over the right because it maximises blood flow to the placenta and kidneys.

Don't panic if you wake up on your back — simply roll back to your left side. The risk is associated with sustained back sleeping, not brief periods of rolling over in sleep. A good pregnancy pillow (more on those below) can help train your body to stay on its side throughout the night.

First Trimester Sleep Strategies

Embrace daytime napping

In the first trimester, your body is doing extraordinary work — building a placenta, a new circulatory system, and all of your baby's major organs. The fatigue you feel is real and physiological. Rather than fighting it, work with it. Short naps of 20-30 minutes in the early afternoon can significantly improve your energy levels and nighttime sleep quality. Avoid napping after 3pm, as this can make it harder to fall asleep at night.

Manage nausea at bedtime

Nausea is often worst in the evening for many pregnant women. Eating a small, bland snack before bed — plain crackers, a small piece of toast, or a few almonds — can help stabilise blood sugar overnight and reduce nausea. Keep a packet of crackers beside your bed to eat before you get up in the morning, which can prevent the worst of morning nausea and make it easier to return to sleep after nighttime bathroom trips.

Handle frequent urination

The urge to urinate frequently is one of the earliest and most disruptive pregnancy symptoms. While you can't eliminate it, you can minimise nighttime trips by front-loading your fluid intake — drinking most of your daily water in the morning and afternoon, then reducing fluids in the two hours before bed. A small nightlight in the bathroom means you don't have to turn on bright lights, which can make it much easier to fall back asleep after a bathroom trip.

Second Trimester Sleep Strategies

Start sleeping on your left side now

Even if you're still comfortable sleeping on your back in the second trimester, now is the ideal time to train yourself to sleep on your left side — before your bump is large enough to make back sleeping uncomfortable anyway. The earlier you establish the habit, the easier it will be later.

Introduce a pregnancy pillow

A good pregnancy pillow is genuinely one of the most impactful purchases you can make for your sleep during pregnancy. They provide support for your bump, back, hips, and knees simultaneously — addressing the core physical discomforts that disrupt sleep. The difference between sleeping without and with a quality pregnancy pillow is remarkable.

A U-shaped or C-shaped full body pregnancy pillow supports your bump, back, hips and knees all at once. Widely recommended by midwives and physiotherapists. One of the most popular pregnancy purchases for good reason — mamas who use them consistently report dramatically better sleep.

Address back pain early

Back pain often begins in the second trimester as the growing uterus shifts your centre of gravity and places increased strain on your lumbar spine. Address it early rather than waiting for it to become debilitating. Prenatal yoga, swimming, and regular gentle walking all help. A supportive mattress topper can also make a significant difference if your current mattress is too firm or too soft for pregnancy comfort.

Third Trimester Sleep Strategies

The third trimester is where most pregnant women struggle most with sleep. Here are the most effective strategies for each specific challenge:

For heartburn disrupting sleep

Heartburn worsens significantly in the third trimester as your growing uterus pushes upward on your stomach. Sleeping with your upper body slightly elevated — using a wedge pillow or an extra pillow under your mattress at the head end — can dramatically reduce nighttime heartburn. Avoid eating in the two hours before bed, eat smaller meals throughout the day rather than large ones, and avoid spicy, fatty, or acidic foods in the evening.

Wedge Pillow for Pregnancy

A pregnancy wedge pillow elevates your upper body to reduce heartburn and can also be used to support your bump when sleeping on your side. Much more versatile than it looks — many mamas use it throughout the third trimester and postpartum for feeding support too.

For restless legs syndrome

Restless legs syndrome (RLS) — an uncomfortable urge to move your legs, particularly at night — affects up to 26% of pregnant women and is most common in the third trimester. Iron deficiency is a common trigger, so ask your midwife to check your iron levels if you're experiencing RLS. Magnesium supplementation has also been shown to help — discuss with your healthcare provider. Gentle leg stretches before bed, warm baths, and keeping the bedroom cool can provide some relief.

For anxiety and a racing mind

Third trimester anxiety about birth, the baby's health, and the upcoming life change is extremely common and can cause significant sleep disruption. A consistent pre-sleep wind-down routine is one of the most effective tools available. This might include a warm shower, gentle stretching, a few pages of a non-stimulating book, and 5-10 minutes of slow breathing or a guided meditation.

Sleep Headphones / Headband

Comfortable sleep headphones in a soft headband allow you to listen to guided meditations, sleep music, or white noise without disturbing your partner. Particularly helpful for mamas who find racing thoughts the main barrier to sleep. Soft and flat enough to wear comfortably while lying on your side.

For general discomfort and waking

By the third trimester, simply rolling over in bed requires significant effort and often fully wakes you up. Satin or silk pyjamas and a satin pillowcase reduce the friction of moving in bed, making position changes much easier and less likely to fully wake you. Keeping the bedroom cool (around 18-20°C) also helps, as pregnant women often run warmer than usual due to increased metabolic rate.

Building a sleep-friendly environment

Your sleep environment has a profound impact on sleep quality — and this is especially true during pregnancy when sleep is already fragile. Key environmental factors to optimise:

Temperature

The ideal bedroom temperature for sleep is 18-20°C. During pregnancy, you may need it even cooler due to the increased heat generated by your growing baby and elevated metabolism. A fan can help with both temperature regulation and white noise.

Light

Light — particularly blue light — suppresses melatonin production and delays sleep onset. Avoid screens (phone, tablet, television) for at least an hour before bed. Use blackout curtains or a sleep mask to keep the room dark. Install an amber-toned nightlight in the bathroom for nighttime bathroom trips — this minimally disrupts melatonin compared to white or blue light.

🌙

White Noise Machine

A white noise machine creates a consistent ambient sound that masks disruptive noises — a snoring partner, traffic, neighbours — and creates a relaxing sleep cue. Many white noise machines also offer nature sounds, rain, and pink or brown noise. An excellent investment for pregnancy sleep and beyond — your newborn will also benefit from white noise in those early weeks.

Magnesium for sleep

Magnesium glycinate is one of the most well-supported natural sleep supplements, and it's also beneficial during pregnancy for reducing leg cramps, supporting the nervous system, and improving mood. Many pregnant women are deficient in magnesium due to increased demands. Speak to your midwife or doctor before starting any supplement, but magnesium glycinate is generally considered safe and beneficial during pregnancy.

Magnesium Flakes Bath Soak

A warm bath with magnesium flakes in the hour before bed is a deeply relaxing pre-sleep ritual that also delivers magnesium transdermally. Safe during pregnancy, helps reduce leg cramps and restless legs, and supports the nervous system for calmer sleep. Not too hot — warm is the goal. A wonderful self-care ritual for the third trimester.

Your pre-sleep routine

A consistent pre-sleep routine signals to your nervous system that sleep is approaching and helps your body transition from the alertness of daytime to the rest state required for sleep. Even a simple 20-30 minute routine can make a significant difference. A routine that works well for many pregnant mamas:

  1. 9pm: Last food and fluids for the evening. Begin dimming lights in the home.
  2. 9:30pm: Warm shower or bath — the drop in body temperature after a warm bath naturally promotes sleepiness.
  3. 9:50pm: Apply belly oil or moisturiser — a gentle self-care ritual that supports skin elasticity and promotes relaxation.
  4. 10pm: In bed — read a physical book (not a screen) or listen to a guided meditation or sleep story.
  5. 10:15pm: Lights out, white noise on, phone face-down.

When to talk to your midwife or doctor

Some sleep problems during pregnancy warrant medical attention. Speak to your healthcare provider if you experience:

  • Loud snoring or being told you stop breathing in sleep — sleep apnoea is more common during pregnancy and increases health risks
  • Severe restless legs that don't respond to self-care strategies
  • Persistent insomnia that is significantly impacting your mental health and daily functioning
  • Itching that wakes you — persistent itching, particularly of the hands and feet, can indicate obstetric cholestasis, which requires medical evaluation

Poor sleep during pregnancy is common, but it doesn't have to be your normal. With the right strategies, environment, and support, meaningful improvement is achievable — and you and your baby deserve rest. 💚
