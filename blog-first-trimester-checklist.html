✅ Pregnancy Planning

First Trimester
Checklist

Everything you need to do, buy, arrange, and know in the first 12 weeks of pregnancy — a complete, practical checklist.

The first trimester — weeks 1 through 12 — is both the most critical and the most uncertain period of pregnancy. Your baby's major organs are forming, your body is undergoing enormous hormonal shifts, and you're likely navigating the practical realities of early pregnancy while the news still feels new. This comprehensive checklist covers everything you need to do, arrange, and know in these first crucial weeks.

"The first trimester is when the foundations are laid — for your baby's development, your prenatal care, and your pregnancy mindset. Getting the basics right now makes everything easier later."

Medical appointments and tests

Confirm your pregnancy and find a care provider

If you've had a positive home pregnancy test, book an appointment with your GP as soon as possible to confirm the pregnancy with a blood test (which measures hCG levels more accurately than a urine test) and to establish your care pathway. In most countries you'll need to register with a midwife, OB-GYN, or maternity hospital early to secure your preferred care option — popular practices and hospitals can fill up quickly.

First trimester screening (weeks 10-13)

First trimester screening typically involves a nuchal translucency ultrasound and a blood test that together assess the risk of chromosomal conditions including Down syndrome, Edwards syndrome, and Patau syndrome. This screening is optional — it's important to understand that it provides risk information rather than a diagnosis, and to decide in advance how you'll proceed with different results. Discuss this with your partner and care provider before the appointment.

  • ☐ Book GP appointment to confirm pregnancy
  • ☐ Register with midwife or obstetrician
  • ☐ Book first trimester screening (10-13 weeks)
  • ☐ Confirm existing medications are safe with your doctor
  • ☐ Book dental check-up — dental health is important in pregnancy

Supplements and nutrition

Start prenatal vitamins immediately

If you haven't already started a prenatal vitamin, begin now. The most critical nutrients in the first trimester are folate (or methylfolate), iodine, and vitamin D. Many standard prenatal vitamins contain synthetic folic acid — opt for methylfolate if possible, as it's better absorbed by most people.

Nutrition in the first trimester

If nausea is limiting what you can eat, focus on what you can tolerate rather than perfect nutrition. Most babies are not nutritionally compromised by first trimester nausea — your prenatal vitamin covers the critical micronutrients. Key things to focus on when you can eat well:

  • Folate-rich foods: leafy greens, lentils, fortified cereals, citrus fruits
  • Iron-rich foods with vitamin C for absorption
  • Protein at every meal to support rapid cell division
  • Filtered water — at least 2 litres daily
  • Ginger in any form for nausea management

Lifestyle changes to make now

Stop immediately

  • ☐ Alcohol — no safe amount has been established in pregnancy
  • ☐ Smoking — seek support from your GP if you need help quitting
  • ☐ Recreational drugs
  • ☐ High-mercury fish (shark, swordfish, king mackerel)
  • ☐ Raw fish, raw shellfish, and undercooked meat
  • ☐ Unpasteurised dairy and soft cheeses
  • ☐ Excessive caffeine — limit to 200mg per day maximum

Start or continue

  • ☐ Prenatal vitamins with methylfolate
  • ☐ Gentle daily exercise — walking, swimming, prenatal yoga
  • ☐ Adequate sleep — your body is doing extraordinary work
  • ☐ Stress management — meditation, journalling, therapy if needed
  • ☐ Dental hygiene — pregnancy hormones increase gum disease risk

Practical planning and preparation

Financial planning

The first trimester is a good time to review your financial situation and begin planning for the costs ahead. Research your maternity/paternity leave entitlements at work, understand what government parental payments you may be eligible for, and start building a baby budget. The earlier you start saving and planning, the less financial stress you'll experience in the later stages of pregnancy.

Insurance and legal

  • ☐ Review health insurance coverage for pregnancy and birth
  • ☐ Check life insurance — consider updating beneficiaries
  • ☐ Review your will — many couples update their wills when expecting
  • ☐ Understand your employment rights during pregnancy

Workplace considerations

You are not legally required to tell your employer you are pregnant during the first trimester — most people wait until after the 12-week scan when miscarriage risk decreases significantly. However, if your job involves physical risk factors (heavy lifting, chemical exposure, night shifts), you may need to inform your employer earlier so that appropriate risk assessments and adjustments can be made.

Emotional wellbeing in the first trimester

The first trimester is emotionally complex for many women. Excitement and joy are often mixed with anxiety, ambivalence, and the very real awareness that early pregnancy carries risks. Up to 15-20% of recognised pregnancies end in miscarriage, predominantly in the first trimester, which can make it difficult to fully invest emotionally until after the 12-week mark.

There is no right way to feel about your pregnancy in the first trimester. Some women feel deeply connected from the moment of the positive test; others feel detached or anxious until they see the heartbeat on an ultrasound. Both are completely normal. If anxiety is significantly affecting your daily functioning, please speak to your GP or midwife — perinatal mental health support is available and highly effective.

Telling people your news

The traditional advice to wait until 12 weeks to announce your pregnancy reflects the statistical reality that miscarriage risk drops significantly after this point. However, this is entirely a personal decision. Some couples choose to tell close family and trusted friends early so they have support if a miscarriage does occur. Others prefer to wait until after screening results. There is no right answer — do what feels right for you and your partner.
