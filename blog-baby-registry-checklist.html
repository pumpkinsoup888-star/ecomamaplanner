The Eco Mama Baby
Registry Checklist

Cut through the marketing noise — here's exactly what you actually need for a new baby, and what you can skip entirely.

The baby products industry is extraordinarily good at convincing new parents that they need an enormous array of products, gadgets, and accessories to be good parents and keep their baby safe. The reality is that babies need very few things — and many of the products marketed most aggressively to expectant parents are unnecessary, quickly outgrown, or actively unhelpful.

This eco-friendly baby registry checklist cuts through the noise. It covers what you genuinely need, what's optional but helpful, and — importantly — what you can safely skip entirely, saving both money and the environmental cost of unnecessary manufacturing and disposal.

"The best baby registry focuses on quality over quantity — a few excellent essentials rather than a room full of gadgets that collect dust after two weeks."

The absolute essentials — must haves

These are the items that every baby genuinely needs, regardless of budget or parenting philosophy.

Safe sleep setup

Your baby will spend more time sleeping than doing anything else in the first year — up to 18 hours per day in the newborn period. A safe sleep setup is the most important investment you'll make.

  • Cot or bassinet — always buy new or check second-hand against current safety standards
  • New organic cot mattress — always buy new, firm, and flat. An organic mattress eliminates exposure to flame retardant chemicals and off-gassing foam
  • 3-4 fitted sheets — organic cotton is softer, more durable, and free from pesticide residues
  • 2-3 swaddle blankets — muslin swaddles are lightweight, breathable, and multi-purpose

Feeding supplies

What you need here depends significantly on whether you plan to breastfeed, formula feed, or combination feed. Breastfeeding requires the least equipment but can benefit from some key supports.

  • Nursing bras (2-3) — sized after 36 weeks when your size is most predictable
  • Breast pump — an electric double pump is most efficient if you plan to return to work
  • Nursing pads — reusable organic cotton pads are eco-friendly and economical
  • Nipple cream — pure lanolin or organic coconut oil
  • Nursing pillow — supports positioning and reduces arm fatigue
  • Glass bottles (4-6) — necessary even for breastfed babies as an occasional backup

Nappies and changing

  • Nappies — cloth (recommended) or eco-disposable for the newborn period while you find your feet
  • Wipes — reusable cloth wipes for home, biodegradable disposables for out
  • Nappy cream — a natural zinc oxide cream for rash prevention
  • Changing mat — waterproof, wipeable, and portable
  • Wet bags — for storing used cloth nappies and wet items when out

Clothing

Babies grow extraordinarily fast — a newborn may wear the same size for as little as two weeks. Buy minimally for each size and focus on quality over quantity.

  • 6-8 onesies per size (newborn and 0-3 months to start)
  • 4-6 sleepsuits/rompers per size
  • 2-3 warm layers for the relevant season
  • Hats (newborns lose heat rapidly through their heads)

Buy organic cotton where possible — it's more durable than conventional cotton, meaning it washes better and lasts through more children. Buying second-hand for all clothing except underwear is completely safe and dramatically reduces cost and environmental impact.

The very helpful extras

These items are not strictly essential but most parents find them significantly improve daily life with a newborn.

Baby carrier or wrap

A quality baby carrier is one of the best investments for new parents. Babywearing keeps your baby close (beneficial for bonding, breastfeeding, and settling), frees your hands for other activities, and is associated with reduced crying. Ring slings are excellent for newborns; structured carriers become more comfortable as babies gain head control.

Baby monitor

Essential once your baby sleeps in a separate room. A basic audio monitor is sufficient for most families — video monitors are convenient but not necessary. Low-EMF options are available for parents who prefer to minimise electromagnetic field exposure near sleeping babies.

White noise machine

White noise replicates the sounds of the womb and is highly effective at settling newborns and extending sleep. A dedicated white noise machine is safer than leaving a phone or tablet playing near a sleeping baby, and many models also function as nightlights.

What you can skip entirely

The following items are marketed heavily to new parents but are rarely worth purchasing:

  • Nappy bin/pail — a regular bin with a lid works perfectly
  • Baby bath — bathing a newborn in a clean washing-up bowl or on a folded towel in the regular bath is completely fine
  • Changing table — a changing mat on the floor is safer and more versatile
  • Wipe warmer — unnecessary and a fire hazard
  • Baby food processor — a regular blender or hand blender works identically
  • Elaborate nursery decor — your newborn cannot see clearly beyond 30cm and has no interest in nursery themes
  • Newborn shoes — babies cannot walk, cannot feel the ground through shoes, and their feet need to move freely

Building your eco registry

When building your registry, focus on products that are: durable enough to last through multiple children, made from natural materials where possible, multi-functional rather than single-purpose, available in neutral colours that work for any gender, and from brands with transparent supply chains and ethical manufacturing practices.

Consider adding a note to your registry explaining your eco values — most friends and family will be happy to support your choices when they understand them. Alternatively, ask for contributions to a nappy fund rather than specific products, giving you flexibility to choose what works best for your family.
