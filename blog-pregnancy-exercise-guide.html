The Complete Pregnancy
Exercise Guide

Safe, effective workouts for every stage of your pregnancy — plus everything you need to know about exercising safely for you and your baby.

Exercise during pregnancy is one of the most beneficial things you can do for both your own health and your baby's development. Yet many pregnant women either avoid exercise entirely out of fear, or continue their pre-pregnancy routine without adapting it for the significant changes happening in their bodies. This guide covers everything you need to know to exercise safely, effectively, and enjoyably throughout all three trimesters.

"Exercise in pregnancy is not about maintaining your pre-pregnancy fitness — it's about supporting your changing body, preparing for birth, and investing in a faster recovery."

Why exercise during pregnancy matters

The evidence base for exercise in pregnancy is extensive and overwhelmingly positive. Regular moderate exercise during pregnancy is associated with lower rates of gestational diabetes, preeclampsia, excessive weight gain, back pain, and depression. It improves sleep quality, reduces fatigue, builds the stamina needed for labour, and is associated with faster postpartum recovery. Babies of mothers who exercise regularly during pregnancy tend to have healthier birth weights and better cardiovascular health markers.

The old advice to "rest and take it easy" during pregnancy has been thoroughly overturned by modern research. Current guidelines from major obstetric organisations worldwide recommend at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity exercise per week throughout pregnancy for women without contraindications — the same recommendation as for non-pregnant adults.

Exercise safety — the essential rules

Before starting or continuing an exercise program in pregnancy, always discuss it with your midwife or doctor. Most women can exercise safely throughout pregnancy, but there are certain conditions where exercise may need to be modified or avoided — including placenta praevia, preterm labour risk, severe anaemia, and certain heart conditions.

Signs to stop exercising immediately

Stop exercising and contact your healthcare provider if you experience: vaginal bleeding or fluid leakage, chest pain or palpitations, severe shortness of breath, dizziness or feeling faint, severe headache, calf pain or swelling, decreased fetal movement, or painful contractions. These symptoms require medical evaluation before resuming exercise.

General safety principles

  • Stay hydrated — drink water before, during, and after exercise
  • Avoid overheating — exercise in cool environments, avoid hot yoga or hot water exercise
  • Wear supportive footwear — your centre of gravity changes in pregnancy, increasing injury risk
  • Listen to your body — reduce intensity if you can't hold a conversation
  • Avoid lying flat on your back after the first trimester — this can compress the vena cava
  • Warm up and cool down — pregnancy hormones make joints more lax and prone to injury

First Trimester Exercise — Weeks 1-12

The first trimester is when many women feel most cautious about exercise — and when the symptoms of pregnancy (nausea, fatigue, breast tenderness) can make it most challenging. The good news is that if you were exercising before pregnancy, you can generally continue at the same level, with a few modifications.

Best exercises for the first trimester

  • Walking — the safest and most accessible exercise throughout all of pregnancy. Even a 20-minute daily walk provides significant benefits.
  • Swimming — zero impact on joints, naturally keeps you cool, and the water pressure provides gentle support for your growing uterus.
  • Prenatal yoga — builds flexibility, practices breathing techniques for labour, and provides mental wellness benefits alongside physical ones.
  • Light strength training — maintaining muscle mass supports posture and reduces back pain throughout pregnancy.
  • Cycling on a stationary bike — removes fall risk while maintaining cardiovascular fitness.

What to avoid in the first trimester

High-impact exercises with a fall risk (skiing, horseback riding, contact sports), heavy weightlifting that involves breath-holding (Valsalva maneuver), hot yoga or heated exercise environments, and any activity that causes pain or discomfort. If you experience significant nausea, gentle movement — even a slow walk — is often more helpful than rest, as it can reduce nausea through improved circulation.

Second Trimester Exercise — Weeks 13-27

The second trimester is often called the "golden trimester" for exercise — nausea has typically eased, energy levels have improved, and the bump is not yet large enough to significantly restrict movement. Many women find this their most enjoyable period for exercise during pregnancy.

Key modifications from week 20

From around 20 weeks, avoid exercises that require lying flat on your back for extended periods. The growing uterus can compress the inferior vena cava in this position, reducing blood return to the heart. Instead, modify floor exercises to a side-lying position or use a wedge to prop up your upper body. Listen to your body — brief periods on your back during transitions are generally fine, but sustained back-lying should be avoided.

Begin introducing pelvic floor exercises (Kegels) if you haven't already. The pelvic floor supports the weight of your growing uterus and will be under significant stress during labour. Regular Kegels strengthen these muscles and are associated with reduced risk of perineal tearing and faster postpartum recovery.

Third Trimester Exercise — Weeks 28-40

Exercise in the third trimester requires the most modification but remains highly beneficial. The growing bump affects balance and centre of gravity, making fall-risk activities more dangerous. Discomforts including round ligament pain, pelvic girdle pain, and shortness of breath may require exercise intensity to be reduced.

Focus areas for third trimester

  • Pelvic floor exercises — daily Kegels and diaphragmatic breathing prepare for labour and postpartum recovery
  • Gentle walking — shorter, more frequent walks may be more comfortable than longer ones
  • Swimming — the water supports your bump weight, making this increasingly comfortable as pregnancy advances
  • Prenatal yoga focusing on hip opening — cat-cow, child's pose, and figure-four stretches prepare your hips for labour
  • Birthing ball exercises — gentle rocking, hip circles, and sitting on a birthing ball encourage optimal fetal positioning

Pelvic floor exercises — the most important exercise in pregnancy

If you do only one type of exercise throughout your entire pregnancy, make it pelvic floor exercises. The pelvic floor is a hammock of muscles at the base of your pelvis that supports your bladder, uterus, and bowel. During pregnancy it supports the increasing weight of your growing baby. During labour it plays a critical role in the birthing process. After birth, its strength determines how quickly you recover from birth-related symptoms including incontinence.

To perform a Kegel: identify your pelvic floor muscles by imagining you're trying to stop the flow of urine. Gently lift and squeeze these muscles, hold for 5-10 seconds, then release completely. Repeat 10-15 times, three times per day. The "release" is as important as the squeeze — a pelvic floor that is too tight is as problematic as one that is too weak.

Exercise after a difficult pregnancy

For women experiencing pregnancy complications — pelvic girdle pain, symphysis pubis dysfunction (SPD), diastasis recti, or high-risk pregnancy — standard exercise guidelines may not apply. Always consult a women's health physiotherapist who specialises in pregnancy and postpartum if you have any specific concerns. These specialists can provide individualised exercise programs that address your specific needs while keeping both you and your baby safe.
